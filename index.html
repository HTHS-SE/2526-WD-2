Overview

Impressionism was born in 1874 in a gallery organized by a group of artists called the Anonymous Society of Painters, Sculptors, Printmakers etc. The founding members included the likes of Claude Monet, Pierre-August Renoir, Edgar Degas, and Alfred Sisley, who together made up a diverse group of creatives. Despite their differences, they were unified by their independence from the conservative Salon exhibition.

Though these artists differed in individual style, their shared techniques (such as loose, feathered brushwork) and colorful depictions of everyday life became hallmarks of the Impressionist movement. Such an approach may seem cliche in the modern age, but it was revolutionary at the time; paintings were expected to depict historical and biblical subjects in more muted tones and with rigid techniques. Indeed, art critic Louis Leroy accused Claude Monet’s Impression, Sunrise of being merely an “impression” or a sketch rather than a complete painting, giving the movement its name.

Nevertheless, despite its unfavorable initial reception, Impressionism continues to have a lasting influence on the art of today. Whether through sparking the Avant-Garde movement through its challenging of artistic norms or broadening the scope of art to include natural, fleeting scenes, Impressionism’s legacy can be seen throughout modern paintings, photography, and culture.
