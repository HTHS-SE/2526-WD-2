Timeline

An overview of key events in the history of Impressionism

Collapsible Metal Paint Tube

1841

John Goffe Rand, an American inventor, patented his invention for collapsible metal paint tubes in 1841. Before this, paint was very difficult to make and transport, and painting in outside areas was very impractical. Without this invention, most Impressionist artist would have never gotten started. As Impressionist painter Renoir said, "Without colours in tubes, there would be no Cezanne, no Monet, no Pissarro, and no Impressionism."

A Burial at Ornans

1849

Gustave Courbet started the Realism movement with his 1849 painting A Burial at Ornans. Realism, as the name suggests, is the practice of only painting real moments. Courbet's painting started the shift from the Romanticism era to a focus on everyday life and ordinary people.

A Burial at Ornans, 1849

Pavilion of Realism

1855

After many of his paintings had been rejected for display at the Salon (the state-sponsored annual art exhibition), Courbet decided to take matters into his own hands. He organized his own exhibition, the "Pavilion of Realism," in response to the art world's rejection of Realism.

Pavilion of Realism, 1855

Avant-Garde Artists Accumulate

1862

In 1862, young artists Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Alfred Sisley, and Frederic Bazille are all studying in Paris in the same studio. This particular group would go on to push the boundaries of Parisian art and be the founders of the Impressionist movement.

Manet's Olympia

1865

Considered one of the first modern paintings, Manet's painting Olympia generated significant controversy when shown at the year's Salon. In the painting, Manet used a stark contrast between light and shadow, as well as an unconventional composition, to challenge traditional representations of the female nude and create an illusion of a 3D form.

Impression Begins

1869

The Impressionist movement began to take shape as artists like Monet and Renoir started to experiment with light and color in new ways. Specifically, Monet and Renoir painted the middle class and their leisure activities at La Grenouillère. Here, they used short and rapid strokes, a trademark of Impressionism.

Société anonyme coopérative des artistes peintres, sculpteurs et graveurs

1873

The Société anonyme coopérative des artistes peintres, sculpteurs et graveurs was founded in 1873 by a group of artists including Monet, Renoir, and Degas. This cooperative aimed to provide a platform for artists to exhibit their work outside the traditional Salon system, which was often restrictive and biased against innovative styles like Impressionism.

The First Impressionist Exhibition

1874

The first Impressionist exhibition took place in 1874, organized by the Société anonyme coopérative des artistes peintres, sculpteurs et graveurs. This groundbreaking event showcased works by Monet, Renoir, Degas, and others, marking a significant departure from the traditional Salon exhibitions. There would continue to be a number of Impressionist exhibition all throughout the 1870s and 1880s as the style grew in popularity.
